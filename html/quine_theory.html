The Quine-McCluskey Technique

What is The Quine-McCluskey Technique?


The Quine-McCluskey technique developed Edward J. McCluskey and Willard V. Quine is yet another method of simplifying Boolean functions. However, this method is better preferred than the Karnaugh Map Method as it is much easier to simplify Boolean functions which have more than 5 variables.

This method utilizes a tabular method using prime implicants, where a prime implicant is a product or sum term which cannot be reduced any further even by combining any other product or sum terms from the Boolean function.

Next you will compare numbers in group Red or group 1 with those in Group Yellow or Group 2. You will look for the numbers where there is only one bit different when comparing. That different bit would be omitted and the remaining would be the result.

For example, with W'X'YZ' (0010) in the Red Group that would compare with W'XYZ'- 0110 in the 2nd group and as they have only one bit different which is the X and X' that would be omitted, and it would leave W'YZ'.

Here you would do the same as you did for the 1st table where you compare elements in the 1st group or Red group with those in the Yellow group or 2nd group, then the Yellow group with the Blue Group.

Step 1

For the first step the terms are arranged in the table from the term with the most compliments to the term with the least compliments. The binary equivalent of the terms are then represented in the respective A,B,C and D columns (A 1 for an uncomplimented term and a 0 for a complimented term). The index column is then used to store the decimal equivalent of the binary term.

After this is done, starting from the top you check the terms binary form and look for a SINGLE variable change. If this change is found, you then place a tick in the furthest column showing that the element was matched to another.


Step 2

In this step, you will now combine the terms that matched with one another for each instance leaving out the term that is different.

A'BC'D + A'BCD = ABD

ABC'D + ABCD = ABD

In this step, you will now repeat the process done in Step 1. However, this time with a few changes, you will now use the terms found in Step 2 as your product terms. Now that we have reached the final step, let's explain what happens here. The column headings are each of the individual terms from the original expression, the row headings are the terms which we marked with a “X” during our work with the other tables. Now, an X is placed wherever the row element is apart of the column element. Afterwards, whenever an X is in a column by itself changed to a circle. Also, whenever an X is in the same row as circle, that X is changed to a square. Finally, your simplified expression will consist of a function made up of the terms represented by the Circled Terms.

This being said, the final answer is :A'BC + ACD + A'BC + A'C'D
